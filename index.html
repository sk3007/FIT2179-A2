Section 1 of 4 — Causes

Where Do Malaysia's Emissions Come From?

Deforestation is a major contributor to Malaysia's emissions, with significant tree-cover loss across all states, especially in Sarawak, Sabah and Pahang.

Emissions of pollutant gases have surged in Malaysia after World War II, with the emissions of pollutant gases such as Carbon Monoxide (CO) and Methane (CH₄) rising significantly, by up to 1600% compared to pre-war levels.

Section 2 of 4 — Climate Observations

The Climate Is Already Changing: Heat, Rain, and Rising Seas

Starting from the 2010s, Malaysia has experienced more hot days
(>35°C) than before. The effect is especially obvious during the
intermonsoon months from April to June.

The total amount of rainfall has increased along with higher
mean surface temperatures and longer El Niño events, leading to
more climate disasters and rising sea levels.

Section 3 of 4 — Effects

From Weather Events to Human Costs: Floods, Disease, and Falling Harvests

Floods and Disasters: Displacement Across Malaysia

Flood impacts are becoming more frequent and severe. While rainfall no longer follows ENSO patterns, flood displacement continues to rise, especially along the east coast states of peninsula Malaysia such as Kelantan, Terengganu and Pahang. Recently, Kota Bharu recorded the largest event in 2024, displacing 101,319 people, despite preventive measures, clearly showing the toll of climate change on human livelihoods.

Disaster-related economic losses has increased by around 700% over the past 20 years. Major flood years such as 2015 and 2021 show how extreme events can exceed the long-term trend, with damages projected to reach about USD 1.6 billion within two decades.

Section 4 of 4 — Outlook & Mitigation

Deliberate Action Is Already Changing the Trajectory

Power-Station Geography: Renewable vs. Non-Renewable

Renewable power stations are clustered mainly in northern Peninsular Malaysia and East Malaysia, where hydro, biomass and solar resources are more prominent. Since 2010, renewable production has grown rapidly from below 1,000 MW to over 4,000 MW.

Even so, renewables account for only about 22% of Malaysia's total electricity generation. This shows that while renewable infrastructure is expanding, Malaysia's power sector remains heavily dependent on non-renewable sources. Moving forward, Malaysia has to harness its renewable potential and accelerate the transition to cleaner energy to meet its climate goals.

Economic Diversification

Between 2010 and 2017, Malaysia's economy shifted toward services (rising from 51.2% to 54.5%) while heavy carbon-emitting sectors like mining and manufacturing contracted. To future-proof green economic growth, Malaysia must leverage its competitive advantages in technology and services, while investing in sustainable industries and workforce development, to accelerate this structural change to a low-carbon economy.
